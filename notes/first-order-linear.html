    Section 1 Solution Technique 1: First Order Linear ODEs

    This note gives two useful techniques for solving first-order linear ODEs.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1. First-Order Linear ODE (most of Definition 2.1.1 in Text).

    A first-order ODE that can be written in the form
    \begin{equation} u' = g(t) + h(t)u\tag{1.1} \end{equation}
    for some functions \(g(t)\) and \(h(t)\) is called linear. If \(g(t)\equiv 0\text{,}\) then the linear ODE is homogeneous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Integrating Factor Method

    The following method will always work to find solutions to linear first-order ODEs. The only potential problem with it is finding antiderivatives. Because of the name of the function \(\mu(t)\) in this method, it is called the integrating factor method.
    🔗

    Solution Method 1.2. Solving with an Integrating Factor.

    To solve (1.1), proceed as follows:
    1. Re-write the equation in the form
      \begin{equation*} u' - h(t)u = g(t). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Compute the integrating factor
      \begin{equation*} \mu(t) = e^{-\int h(t)dt} \end{equation*}
      (the integral in the exponent can me taken with constant of integration equal to zero). Observe that \(\mu'(t) = -h(t)\mu(t) \text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Multiply both sides of the re-written ODE by \(\mu(t)\) to obtain
      \begin{equation*} u'\mu(t) - h(t)\mu(t)u(t) = g(t)\mu(t). \end{equation*}
      Note that, by the observation in the previous step, the left-hand side of this equation is
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}(u\cdot \mu(t)). \end{equation*}
      Thus, the ODE takes the form
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}(u\cdot \mu(t)) = g(t)\mu(t). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Integrate both sides of the last equation to get rid of the \(\frac{d}{dt}\text{,}\) then solve for \(u(t)\text{.}\) Apply whatever initial conditions you may have to solve for the unknown constant in your solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The \(u_p + u_h\) Method

    Another useful, but less flexible, method relies on the following theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3. Form of Solutions to First-Order Linear ODEs.

    The general solution to the ODE (1.1) is
    \begin{equation*} u(t) = u_h(t) + u_p(t) \end{equation*}
    where \(u_h(t)\) is the general solution to the homogeneous first-order ODE
    \begin{equation} u' = h(t)u\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
    and \(u_p(t)\) is any particular solution to (1.1) you may be able to find.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(u(t)\) be an arbitrary solution to (1.1). Consider the difference \(u(t) - u_p(t)\text{.}\) Observe that
    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}(u(t)-u_p(t)) \amp = (g(t) + h(t)u(t)) - (g(t) + h(t)u_p(t)) \\ \amp = h(t)(u(t) - u_p(t)). \end{align*}
    Hence, \(u(t) - u_p(t)\) is a solution to (1.2), which means \(u(t)\) has the form indicated by the theorem.
    🔗
    🔗

    Solution Method 1.4. The \(u_p + u_h\) Method.

    To solve (1.1), proceed as follows:
    1. Observe that \(u_h(t) = Ke^{\int h(t) dt}\text{,}\) where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Figure out a single solution to (1.1).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Form the general solution as indicated by the theorem and apply any initial conditions to solve for \(K\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.5.

    The technique Solution Method 1.4 is extremely useful when a particular solution can be found by inspection. For instance, when an equilibrium or steady-state solution is evident.
