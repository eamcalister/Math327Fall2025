    Section 2 Solution Technique 2: Separable First-Order ODEs

    This note gives one of the few techniques for solving some non-linear ODEs.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1. Separable First-Order ODE (Definition 2.2.1 in Text).

    A first-order ODE that can be written in the form
    \begin{equation} u' = g(t)h(u)\tag{2.1} \end{equation}
    for some functions \(g(t)\) and \(h(u)\) is called separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Separation of Variables Method

    The following method can be used to solve separable ODEs, at least in principle. The ability to perform the necessary integrations and/or do algebra for an explicit expression for \(u(t)\) will always be an issue (they’re not always possible).
    🔗
    Note: Separation of variables also refers to an important technique for solving partial differential equations using Fourier series. This isn’t what we’re doing.
    🔗

    Solution Method 2.2. Solving with an Integrating Factor.

    To solve (2.1), proceed as follows:
    1. Separate:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{h(u)}du = g(t)dt \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Integrate:
      \begin{equation*} \int\frac{1}{h(u)}du = \int g(t)dt \end{equation*}
      Observe that you are really doing a "\(u\)" substitution on the left-hand side. The integral is really with respect to \(t\text{.}\)
      🔗
      This will get you an equation of the form
      \begin{equation*} F(u) = G(t) + C, \end{equation*}
      where \(G(t)\) is some antiderivative of \(g(t)\) and \(F(u)\) is an antiderivative of \(\frac{1}{F(u)}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Do algebra to solve for \(u(t)\) and/or apply initial conditions to determine \(C\text{.}\) At this point the technique depends on that specific functions you’re dealing with.
      🔗
      🔗
